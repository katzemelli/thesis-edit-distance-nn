Can a neural network learn an algorithm? The orthodox answer is a qualified no. Under the standard reading of the universal-approximation results, a network performs statistical function approximation rather than symbolic computation (**"Multilayer feedforward networks are universal approximators”, - Hornik, Kurt; Stinchcombe, Maxwell; White, Halbert (January 1989))**: it fits a smooth map to the examples it is shown, and there is no procedure inside to be extracted and run as code (**Approximation capabilities of multilayer feedforward networks, Kurt Hornik 1991)**. A second, sharper objection follows from the first. A genuine algorithm, e.g. sorting, is correct across the whole of its valid domain, whereas a trained network is constrained to where it saw data. Off the training distribution, "all bets are off." On this view the very general application that defines an algorithm is exactly the property a learned approximation by a neural network cannot be trusted to have, as it is constraint by what it has seen during the training phase.

For the Levenshtein (edit) distance, the strong form of the first objection has already been settled, and instructively so. **Ohtomo et al. (2025)** constructs a ReLU network that computes Levenshtein Distance *exactly*, by unrolling the dynamic-programming score-matrix recurrence into "matching" and "minimum" modules. The construction is a practical dead end: the network must be rebuilt for every input length (zero-padding changes the distance), its construction cost grows steeply with length (on the order of nineteen minutes at length 100), and the experiments are confined to a binary alphabet. At the end, the neural network at play is much more computationally expensive than the original algorithm itself. Most tellingly, the same network cannot learn by gradient descent, so the output results stalls in local minima. So the exact algorithm can be built into a network but not trained into one.

What remains is therefor the question: Do we even need to exactly model the algorithm or can we drop exactness for a “good enough” approximation? If exactness is dropped, a network does not need to be the algorithm itself. It can instead approximate the function the algorithm computes: The map from a pair of strings to their edit distance, in a form that is cheaper to reuse than running the algorithm it is based on itself. We know that minor inaccuracies are unlikely to cause a problem and are being taken into account in favour for fast methods to compare sequences to each other in the vast databases that have recently become available **(Fast protein structure searching using structure graph embeddings)**.

Hence in the following, we do not claim to recover an exact replica of a runnable edit-distance procedure from a network but rather an approximation. 

Levenshtein Distance has played a central role in sequence alignment in particular and biological database similarity search in general. **(Levenshtein Distance, Sequence Comparison and Biological Database Search, Berger, Waterman, Yu, 2021)**.  The duality between minimum-edit distance and maximum alignment similarity **(Smith & Waterman, 1981)** lets us move freely between a distance and a normalised similarity, which can be used as a label during the training phase of a neural network.

Computing Levenshtein Distance exactly is expensive as we already know: the dynamic program is quadratic, and under the Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis no strongly sub-quadratic algorithm exists **(Backurs & Indyk, 2015).** A metric-preserving embedding trades that per-comparison quadratic cost for a linear-time encoding plus sublinear vector search. This is the embedding principle introduced for edit distance by Dai et al. **(CNN-ED, 2020**) and developed for biological strings by Corso et al. **(NeuroSEED, 2021)**; The neural network discussed in this thesis shares its *deployment shape -* encode once, index, search -  with structure-based retrieval systems such as Progres **(Greener & Jamali, 2025)**, although those occupy a different lane, since their ground truth is structural homology rather than a symbolic distance.

*Side note:*

*The strings used throughout are protein-derived (amino-acid sequences and their secondary-structure and 3Di re-encodings, drawn from CATH), but they enter this work only as a conveniently labelled symbolic corpus: **no biological claim is made**, and the ground truth is always the edit-distance value itself never homology, structure, or function.*

The network built here is a single encoder that maps any symbol string to a fixed-dimensional Euclidean embedding whose geometry preserves Levenshtein edit-distance as a normalised Levenshtein score. The research question is then not whether the procedure can be extracted, but an empirical one: *how far off what the network has seen during training does such a learned approximation of the levenshtein edit distance/similarity actually reach?* How well does it transfer?

The approximation is realised as a learned metric embedding. A Siamese encoder (**Analysing Siamese Neural Network Architectures for Computing Name Similarity.)** maps each string to a 128-dimensional unit vector, trained so that Euclidean distance in that space tracks normalised edit distance. Encoding is linear in length and performed once per string, after which any comparison is a vector operation and retrieval is nearest-neighbour search over an index. 

What separates a learned operation **from memorised dataset statistics is whether it survives leaving the distribution and data it was trained on. The central experiment takes a single encoder, trained only on synthetically perturbed amino-acid strings, and runs it **frozen** in two off-distribution settings: on natural AA-strings (after synthetic-only training), and on symbolic alphabets it never saw*:* secondary structure (3-letter) and the 3Di structural alphabet (20-letter). Were the approximation just memorised amino-acid statistics, it should not transfer. However if it captured an operation that edit distance performs, it should. The result is graded, and that grading is the empirical content: transfer is strong to its natural strings and to a second alphabet. The ceiling is traced to a concrete mechanism (a mismatch between alphabet character-frequency profiles).

This motivates a two-fold claim, stated in the order of priority used throughout the thesis.

- **Primary — retrieval-grade approximation.** The encoder approximates Levenshtein well enough that embedding distance recovers the true high-similarity neighbours, enabling *k-*nearest-neighbour retrieval in place of the SETH-quadratic dynamic program. On the in-distribution amino-acid evaluation this is essentially saturated: the true partner is retrieved in the top ten for every query, at AUROC 0.997.
- **Secondary — off-distribution generalisation.** One frozen encoder transfers across symbolic representations, and from synthetic to natural strings, without retraining. The transfer is partial and frequency-bounded: position-pattern structure carries across alphabets, character-frequency-specific signal does not.

Scope is deliberately narrow. We approximate global, unit-cost Levenshtein (substitution-matrix costs and local alignment are out of scope, noted as future work). We claim approximation of the edit-distance function, not extraction of its procedure.

The comparison set is the family of *e*dit-distance approximators*:* CNN-ED (**Convolutional Embedding for Edit Distance, 2020**), NeuroSEED, and the training-free CGK embedding **(Chakraborty, Goldenberg & Koucký, 2016)**, specifically not biological search tools such as BLAST, Foldseek, or protein language models, which answer a different question against a different ground truth. 

The contributions are three. None is the embedding idea itself (that is CNN-ED's), and none is a biological result:

1. **Off-distribution generalisation as a distinct test.** A single encoder applied without retraining to unseen alphabets, and to natural strings after synthetic-only (https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.07780) training: The algorithm-versus-memorisation probe that the per-dataset CNN-ED / NeuroSEED lineage, which trains and tests on one distribution, never poses.
2. **A mechanistic account of what was learned.** *Position-pattern hashing* as the transfer mechanism (same characters in the same relative positions produce the same embedding, regardless of alphabet), and an *alphabet-frequency-mismatch ceiling* that explains where and why the approximation leaks.
3. **Architectural diagnostics** behind the working encoder: the prediction-compression fix (band-weighted regression replaced by cross-entropy , with within-band ranking preserved geometrically) and the position-rigidity fix (adaptive pooling, **Adaptive Pooling Is All You Need, 2020**).
