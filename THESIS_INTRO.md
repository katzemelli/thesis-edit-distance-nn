Can a neural network *learn an algorithm*? The orthodox answer is a qualified no. Under the standard reading of the universal-approximation results, a network performs statistical *function* approximation rather than symbolic computation: it fits a smooth map to the examples it is shown, and there is no procedure inside to be extracted and run as code. A second, sharper objection follows from the first. A genuine algorithm — sorting, say — is correct across the whole of its valid domain, whereas a trained network is constrained only where it saw data; off the training distribution, "all bets are off." On this view the very generality that *defines* an algorithm is exactly the property a learned approximation cannot be trusted to have.

For the Levenshtein (edit) distance, the strong form of the first objection has already been settled, and instructively so. Ohtomo et al. (2025) construct a ReLU network that computes edit distance *exactly*, by unrolling the dynamic-programming score-matrix recurrence into "matching" and "minimum" modules. The construction is real but a practical dead end: the network must be rebuilt for every input length (zero-padding changes the distance), its construction cost grows steeply with length (on the order of nineteen minutes at length 100), and the experiments are confined to a binary alphabet. Most tellingly, the same network *cannot be learned* by gradient descent, which stalls in local minima. The exact algorithm can be *built* into a network but not *trained* into one. This thesis therefore concedes the no-extraction half of the skeptic's position at the outset: we do not claim to recover a runnable edit-distance procedure from a network.

What remains is the question the concession reframes rather than closes. If exactness is dropped, a network need not *be* the algorithm; it can instead *approximate the function the algorithm computes* — the map from a pair of strings to their edit distance — in a form that is cheap to reuse. The object built here is a single encoder that maps any symbol string to a fixed-dimensional Euclidean embedding whose geometry preserves edit-distance ordering. The live research question is then not whether the procedure can be extracted, but an empirical one: *how far off its training distribution does such a learned approximation actually reach?* That is precisely the second, generalisation half of the skeptic's objection — here posed as something to be measured rather than asserted.

Edit distance is a natural object for this question. It is the historical and conceptual core of sequence comparison (Berger, Waterman & Yu, 2021) and — unlike the alignment *scores* built on top of it — a true *metric*, obeying the triangle inequality; the duality between minimum-edit distance and maximum alignment similarity (Smith & Waterman, 1981) lets us move freely between a distance and a normalised similarity. Computing it exactly is expensive: the dynamic program is quadratic, and under the Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis no strongly sub-quadratic algorithm exists (Backurs & Indyk, 2015). A metric-preserving embedding trades that per-comparison quadratic cost for an amortised linear-time encoding plus sublinear vector search — which is why an approximation, *if it holds*, is worth having. The strings used throughout are protein-derived (amino-acid sequences and their secondary-structure and 3Di re-encodings, drawn from CATH), but they enter this work only as a conveniently labelled symbolic corpus: **no biological claim is made**, and the ground truth is always the edit-distance value itself — never homology, structure, or function.

The approximation is realised as a learned metric embedding. A Siamese encoder maps each string to a 128-dimensional unit vector, trained so that Euclidean distance in that space tracks normalised edit distance; encoding is linear in length and performed once per string, after which any comparison is a vector operation and retrieval is nearest-neighbour search over an index. This is the embedding principle introduced for edit distance by Dai et al. (CNN-ED, 2020) and developed for biological strings by Corso et al. (NeuroSEED, 2021); it shares its *deployment shape* — encode once, index, search — with structure-based retrieval systems such as Progres (Greener & Jamali, 2025), although those occupy a different lane, since their ground truth is structural homology rather than a symbolic distance.

What separates a learned *operation* from memorised dataset statistics is whether it survives leaving the distribution it was trained on. The central experiment takes a single encoder, trained only on synthetically perturbed amino-acid strings, and runs it **frozen** in two off-distribution settings: on *natural* strings (after synthetic-only training), and on *symbolic alphabets it never saw* — secondary structure (3-letter) and the 3Di structural alphabet (20-letter). Were the approximation merely memorised amino-acid statistics, it should not transfer; to the extent it captured the position-sensitive operation that edit distance performs, it should. The result is graded, and that grading is the empirical content: transfer is strong to natural strings and to a second alphabet, and bounded — and the ceiling is traced to a concrete mechanism (a mismatch between alphabet character-frequency profiles) rather than left as noise.

This motivates a two-tier claim, stated in the order of priority used throughout the thesis.

- **Primary — retrieval-grade approximation.** The encoder approximates Levenshtein well enough that embedding distance recovers the true high-similarity neighbours, enabling *k*-nearest-neighbour retrieval in place of the SETH-quadratic dynamic program. On the in-distribution amino-acid evaluation this is essentially saturated: the true partner is retrieved in the top ten for every query, at AUROC 0.997.
- **Secondary — off-distribution generalisation.** One frozen encoder transfers across symbolic representations, and from synthetic to natural strings, without retraining. The transfer is partial and frequency-bounded: position-pattern structure carries across alphabets, character-frequency-specific signal does not.

Scope is deliberately narrow. We approximate *global, unit-cost* Levenshtein (substitution-matrix costs and local alignment are out of scope, noted as future work); we claim approximation of the edit-distance *function*, not extraction of its procedure; and the comparison set is the family of *edit-distance approximators* — CNN-ED, NeuroSEED, and the training-free CGK embedding (Chakraborty, Goldenberg & Koucký, 2016) — not biological search tools such as BLAST, Foldseek, or protein language models, which answer a different question against a different ground truth. Where those tools appear, it is to mark the lane boundary, not to stage a head-to-head.

The contributions are three. None is the embedding idea itself (that is CNN-ED's), and none is a biological result:

1. **Off-distribution generalisation as a distinct test.** A single encoder applied without retraining to unseen alphabets, and to natural strings after synthetic-only training — the algorithm-versus-memorisation probe that the per-dataset CNN-ED / NeuroSEED lineage, which trains and tests on one distribution, never poses.
2. **A mechanistic account of what was learned.** *Position-pattern hashing* as the transfer mechanism (same characters in the same relative positions produce the same embedding, regardless of alphabet), and an *alphabet-frequency-mismatch ceiling* that explains where and why the approximation leaks.
3. **Architectural diagnostics** behind the working encoder: the prediction-compression fix (band-weighted regression replaced by cross-entropy, with within-band ranking preserved geometrically) and the position-rigidity fix (adaptive pooling).
